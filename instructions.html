Refinishing Wood Furniture

When wooden furniture is made it is typically coated with a stain in order to bring out the grain of the wood and tint the wood to a different color as well as a layer of polyurethane in order to provide a glossy protective outer layer. Over the years these coatings tend to degrade and wear down and the top layer of wood can start to rot. Anyone with an old piece of solid wood furniture can refinish it with a just a few simple tools and a little bit of time. Refinishing old furniture can breathe life into old, worn out furniture.

Warning:

Sanding can release extremely fine dust particles that could potentially be harmful when inhaled. Although not required, it is highly recommended that that you wear a N95 approved face safety mask in order to prevent any lung damage.

Equipment

  • Low grit sandpaper (50-80 grit)
  • High grit sandpaper (120-220 grit)
  • Water-Based Polyurethane coating
  • Water-Based Stain
  • Brush
  • Several clean rags
  • Wooden stirring stick
  • Latex gloves
  • N95 approved breathing mask
  1. Initial rough sanding . In order to remove the outer layer of old finish and wood rot we will begin by sanding with our low grit sandpaper.
    1. Fold the sandpaper with the rough side facing outwards into a workable size. Begin by pressing down on the surface of the wood with the rough side of the sandpaper.
    2. Move the sandpaper back and forth in the direction of the wood grain.
      1. The wood grain is the long lines that appear in the texture of the wood.
    3. Continue this motion over the entire surface until the surface is even and free of the old finish and stain. You should be able to see a fresh layer of wood throughout the entire surface.
  2. Removing Scratches . There is a chance you might come across a few scratches in the wood surface while you are sanding.
    1. Begin by positioning your sandpaper on the bottom left section of the scratch and sanding towards the top right section of the scratch. This should form approximately a 30–ç angle between the direction you are sanding and the wood grain.
    2. After several passes, repeat this process beginning from the bottom right section of the scratch and sanding up to the top right section.
    3. After sanding diagonally in both directions, finish by sanding in the direction of the grain and the scratch should be removed.

  3. Fine sanding. After finishing the rough sanding of the surface you will need to smooth out the surface with the high grit sandpaper.
    1. Sand the entire surface just as we did before in step 2. The only difference should be that you are using the high grit sandpaper instead of the low grit.

  4. Prepare the surface for staining. Now that the old finish is completely removed and the surface is smooth, we need to prepare the surface to be stained.
    1. First we must remove the sawdust on the surface. Take a clean rag and slightly dampen it with water.
      1. Be sure not to soak the rag with water.
    2. Gently wipe the surface of the wood with the damp rag until the entire surface is clean of sawdust.
    3. Wait approximately 20 minutes before continuing to ensure that the wood has completely dried.
  5. Staining the surface.
    1. Use the wooden stirring stick to mix the stain and ensure it isnt separated.
    2. Put on latex gloves in order to prevent your hands from being stained.

    6. Caution:

    Wood stain can permanently stain clothing and can be very difficult to remove.

    1. Take one of the rags and rip off a small square.
    2. Dip the rag into a small amount of the stain and be sure not to drench it.
    3. Gently rub the rag into the surface of the wood until the stain is evenly applied.
    4. Reapply stain to the rag as necessary and continue to coat the entire surface of the wood.
    5. Once the entire surface is evenly coated, leave it out to dry. After approximately 8-12 hours, re-apply the stain in the exact same manner using a clean rag. In total there should be about 2 or 3 layers of stain applied, depending on how dark you want the color to be.
  6. Applying polyurethane finish . The final step in finishing the wood is to apply a clear, protective layer of polyurethane.
    1. First check that the stain is completely dry by pressing a white rag to it. If the rag removes some of the stain, then wait several more hours before applying the polyurethane.
    2. Begin by using a wooden stirring stick to mix the polyurethane before beginning.
    3. After it is completely mixed, lightly coat the end of the brush in the polyurethane.
    4. Lightly apply the brush to the surface of the wood using long brush strokes. Be sure you brush in the same direction as the wood grain and you evenly distribute the polyurethane throughout the entire surface.
    5. After the entire surface has been coated, wait 12 hours for the coating to dry and apply one or two more coats using the exact same steps.
      1. The number of polyurethane coatings will dictate how protected the wood will be to wear and weathering.
